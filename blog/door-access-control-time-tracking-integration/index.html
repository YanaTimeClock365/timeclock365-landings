Door Access Control with Time Tracking: The Complete Integration Guide

April 14, 2026 9 min read For HR & Operations Managers

Most engineering and manufacturing companies run two separate systems: a door access control system that logs when employees badge in and out, and a time tracking system where employees clock in and record their hours. On the surface, this seems manageable. In practice, it creates a daily reconciliation problem that costs HR teams hours every week.

An employee badges through the facility door at 7:52 AM, but forgets to clock in on the time tracking terminal until 8:09 AM. The door access log says one thing. The timesheet says another. Someone in HR has to manually resolve the discrepancy — or it silently carries through to payroll as an error.

This guide explains how door access control and time tracking integration works, what to look for in a system that combines both, and how to evaluate whether the operational benefits justify the switch from your current setup.

Why Two Separate Systems Create Problems

Door access control systems were designed for physical security, not workforce management. Their primary job is to grant or deny entry. They log events — "Badge 4471 presented at Door 3 at 07:52:14" — but that data typically lives in a separate system that HR doesn't have easy access to, and it's not formatted for payroll processing.

Time tracking systems, meanwhile, rely on employees to initiate a clock-in event. That might be tapping a terminal, using a mobile app, or entering a PIN. This self-reported step is where errors and abuse enter the system.

The gap between these two systems produces several recurring problems:

  • Manual reconciliation time: When a dispute arises — an employee claims they worked hours that don't appear in the system — HR has to cross-reference the access log against the timesheet manually. In facilities with 100+ employees, this can take hours each pay period.
  • Buddy punching: An employee asks a colleague to clock them in before they actually arrive. The time tracking system records an on-time arrival; the access system would show otherwise — but no one is routinely checking both.
  • Clock-in delays: Employees who badge into the building and then walk to the time clock might clock in 5–10 minutes after their actual arrival. Over a pay period, these small gaps add up.
  • Forgotten clock-outs: Employees who badge out through an access-controlled exit don't always remember to clock out. This leaves open time entries that require manual correction.
  • Data in two places: Finance, HR, and Security are working from different records for the same physical events. Reporting across both is difficult unless someone manually exports and merges the data.

What Integration Actually Means

There are two fundamentally different approaches to integrating door access and time tracking. Understanding the difference matters for your buying decision.

Option 1: API Integration Between Two Separate Systems

In this model, you have two separate software systems — say, a Lenel or Genetec access control system and a Clockify or Hubstaff time tracking system — that are connected via an API. When the access system logs a badge event, it sends a record to the time tracking system, which creates or updates a time entry.

This approach can work, but it has real limitations:

  • Integration quality depends on how well the two vendors' APIs are maintained. Updates to either system can break the connection.
  • You're still paying for two systems, two support contracts, and two sets of administrative overhead.
  • The integration logic — what triggers a clock-in, how conflicts are handled, what happens when badge data is ambiguous — often requires custom configuration that's not well-documented.
  • When something goes wrong, you're stuck in a support triangle between two vendors, each pointing at the other.

Option 2: A Unified System That Does Both

In this model, the door access control hardware and the time tracking software are built as a single product from the ground up. The badge reader is the time clock. When an employee presents their badge at a door, the system simultaneously unlocks the door and records the clock-in event. There is no separate step, no API handoff, no reconciliation needed.

This is the approach TimeClock 365 takes. The door badge event and the time tracking event are the same event, recorded once, in one system.

Key distinction: Integration means connecting two separate things. A unified system means there's only one thing. For most operations teams, the unified approach eliminates more problems because there's no seam between systems where errors can occur.

What to Look for in an Integrated System

If you're evaluating systems that combine door access and time tracking, here are the capabilities that matter most for HR and operations use cases.

1. Automatic Clock-In at Badge Events

The most important feature is that door badge events create time tracking records without any additional employee action. This should be the default behavior, not an optional configuration. Verify specifically: what happens when an employee badges in through an entrance that isn't a primary time-recording point? Are all badge events captured, or only designated ones?

2. Handling of Multi-Door Facilities

Most facilities have multiple entry points — a main entrance, a loading dock, a fire exit that people use as a shortcut. The system needs to handle which doors count as clock-in events and which don't. Look for per-door configuration that lets you designate clock-in/clock-out points clearly.

3. Shift Schedule Integration

Knowing when someone badged in is only useful if you can compare it against when they were scheduled to arrive. The system should allow you to define shifts — start time, end time, department — and flag early arrivals, late arrivals, and early departures against the schedule. This turns raw badge data into actionable attendance information.

4. Overtime and Break Tracking

Time tracking isn't just about start and end of day. Facilities with mandatory break requirements, overtime rules, or split shifts need a system that tracks the full picture. Check whether the system handles: mid-shift breaks (do employees badge out and back in for breaks, or are breaks tracked separately?), overtime thresholds and alerts, and compliance with local labor law requirements for rest periods.

5. Reporting That Replaces the Spreadsheet

One of the main reasons people still use spreadsheets alongside their time tracking system is that the built-in reports don't answer their actual questions. Before selecting a system, define the reports you actually need — daily attendance summary, weekly hours per employee, overtime report, department-level totals — and verify that the system produces them without custom SQL or data exports.

6. Payroll Export

The whole point of accurate time tracking is accurate payroll. Verify what payroll systems the platform integrates with, what the export format looks like, and whether the export requires manual review before processing. A good system should produce a clean payroll file that requires minimal manual intervention.

7. Audit Trail and Dispute Resolution

When an employee disputes their recorded hours, you need a clear audit trail: when did they badge in, when did they badge out, were any manual adjustments made to the record and by whom? The system should log every change with a timestamp and the identity of the person who made it.

Common Implementation Questions

What happens to employees who don't use a badge?

In most facilities, there are contractors, visitors, or remote workers who don't have permanent access badges. A good integrated system handles these cases — typically through a manual clock-in option via supervisor PIN, a mobile check-in for remote workers, or temporary badge assignment for contractors. Clarify how the system handles your specific edge cases before committing.

Do we need to replace our existing door hardware?

This depends on the system. Some integrated platforms are designed to work with standard access control hardware (HID-compatible readers, for example) and can operate alongside existing door controllers with appropriate wiring. Others require proprietary hardware. Ask specifically whether your existing readers are compatible before assuming you'll need a full hardware replacement.

How long does implementation take?

For a single facility with 50–150 employees and standard door hardware, implementation typically runs 2–4 weeks from contract to go-live. Multi-site deployments take longer. The biggest variables are: how complex your shift schedules are, whether you're integrating with an existing HR/payroll system, and whether any door hardware needs to be replaced or rewired.

What about remote and hybrid workers?

Door access integration is specifically a physical facility solution. For employees who work remotely or on a hybrid schedule, a unified system typically offers a secondary clock-in method — a mobile app with geofencing, a web-based clock-in, or supervisor-initiated entries. These remote entries can flow into the same timesheet and reports as the badge-based entries.

The Operational Impact: What Changes After You Integrate

Based on the patterns we see across companies that switch from two separate systems to a unified platform, here's what typically changes:

  • HR administration time: Companies report a significant reduction in time spent on timesheet corrections and payroll disputes — often 3–5 hours per week for operations of 50–200 employees. Most of that time was being spent on manual reconciliation between access logs and time records.
  • Payroll accuracy: The elimination of forgotten clock-ins and buddy punching has a measurable effect on payroll. Companies with shift-based workforces report finding meaningful discrepancies in their first payroll run after switching — hours that were being paid that shouldn't have been, and hours that should have been paid but weren't.
  • Employee behavior: When employees know that their badge swipe is their time record, there's no ambiguity about when the clock starts. This tends to improve punctuality — not because of any punitive enforcement, but because the timing is clear and objective.
  • Dispute resolution: When badge data and time data are the same data, disputes become much easier to resolve. There's one record, with one timestamp. Managers spend less time adjudicating conflicting accounts of when someone arrived.

Is Integration Worth It for Your Organization?

Integration makes the most sense when you have at least some of the following:

  • 50 or more employees at a physical facility
  • Shift-based work schedules
  • Existing door access hardware that could be connected to time tracking
  • Recurring timesheet disputes or payroll corrections
  • HR staff spending significant time on manual reconciliation
  • Any history of buddy punching or attendance manipulation

If your workforce is fully remote or your office has open-door access with no badge requirements, integration won't deliver the same benefits. But for engineering firms, manufacturers, and technical companies with controlled-access facilities, the combination of door access and time tracking in a single system is the most reliable way to produce accurate attendance records without administrative overhead.
