Best Time Tracking Software for Manufacturing Companies: 2026 Comparison

April 1, 2026 10 min read For HR & Operations Managers

The time tracking software market is dominated by tools built for office workers, remote teams, and freelancers. The top-ranked products in most comparison articles — Clockify, Toggl, Harvest, Hubstaff — were designed for people who sit at computers, track time to projects, and clock in via their laptop or phone.

Manufacturing has different requirements. Floor workers don't sit at desks. Shifts start and end at specific times, not when someone decides to start a timer. Clock-in needs to be fast, reliable, and verifiable — not something that relies on an employee remembering to open an app. And critically, most manufacturers already have door access control infrastructure that could, with the right system, eliminate the need for a separate time clock entirely.

This comparison focuses specifically on what matters for manufacturing: physical clock-in mechanisms, shift scheduling, overtime handling, payroll export, and whether the system can integrate with door access control. We've reviewed the leading options with manufacturing use cases in mind.

What Manufacturing Time Tracking Actually Requires

Before the comparison, it's worth being specific about what manufacturing environments need from a time tracking system that office-focused tools don't prioritize.

  • Physical clock-in terminal or badge reader: Floor workers need a fast, physical method to clock in that doesn't depend on a personal smartphone or computer access.
  • Shift scheduling and comparison: The system needs to know when an employee was scheduled to arrive and compare that against when they actually arrived. This isn't optional — it's the foundation of attendance management.
  • Buddy punching prevention: Without identity verification at the clock-in point, shared PINs or badge cards allow time fraud.
  • Overtime calculation rules: Manufacturing often involves complex overtime rules — daily OT thresholds, weekly thresholds, shift differential pay — that need to be configured correctly.
  • Break tracking: Facilities with mandatory break periods need to track compliance, not just total hours.
  • Multi-shift and 24/7 operations: The system needs to handle shift boundaries correctly, including overnight shifts that span calendar days.
  • Payroll export: Clean export to payroll processing — not a spreadsheet that requires manual cleanup.
  • No reliance on personal devices: Floor workers frequently don't carry smartphones on the floor, and requiring a personal device for clock-in creates compliance and coverage issues.

The Tools We Compared

We evaluated seven tools that appear frequently in searches for manufacturing time tracking: TimeClock 365, Clockify, Hubstaff, Deputy, Time Doctor, Rippling, and BambooHR. Here's an honest look at each.

TimeClock 365

Best for Manufacturing

What it is: A purpose-built system that combines door access control and time tracking in a single platform. When an employee badges through a controlled door, the system simultaneously records the access event and the clock-in. There is no separate time clock terminal — the door reader is the time clock.

Key strengths for manufacturing: The core differentiator is that door access and time tracking are the same event. This eliminates buddy punching (you can't clock in for someone without physically using their badge at a controlled door), eliminates forgotten clock-ins (entering the facility is the clock-in), and removes the need to maintain a separate access control system alongside your time tracking system.

The platform handles shift schedules, overtime rules, break tracking, and multi-site deployments. Reporting is built for HR and Operations managers — attendance summaries, hours per employee, overtime flags — not for project billing. Payroll export is included.

Limitations: Primarily designed for facilities with door access control infrastructure. If your facility has completely open access (no badge-controlled doors), you'd need to install readers, which adds hardware cost. Not designed for freelancers or project-based time billing.

Best for: Engineering and manufacturing companies with 50–500 employees, controlled-access facilities, shift-based schedules, and a need to eliminate payroll fraud and manual reconciliation.

Clockify

What it is: A free (with paid tiers) time tracking tool built primarily for project-based time billing. Popular with agencies, consultants, and remote teams.

Manufacturing reality: Clockify is fundamentally a timer-start tool. Employees open the app or web interface and manually start and stop a timer. For office workers tracking billable hours across projects, this is a reasonable workflow. For manufacturing floor workers, it's impractical — workers don't have computer access at the start of their shift, and relying on manual timer starts creates the same problems (forgotten clock-ins, inconsistent records) that a proper time clock system is meant to solve.

Clockify offers a "kiosk" mode — a shared terminal app where multiple employees can clock in with a PIN or QR code. This is more appropriate for manufacturing, but it's a bolt-on feature rather than the core product, and PIN sharing still enables buddy punching. There is no integration with door access control.

Best for: Remote teams, agencies, and consultants tracking time to projects. Not purpose-built for manufacturing floor operations.

Hubstaff

What it is: A time tracking and workforce management tool with GPS tracking, productivity monitoring, and payroll features. Designed for field service teams and remote workforces.

Manufacturing reality: Hubstaff's strongest features are GPS-based location tracking and productivity monitoring (screenshots, app usage). These are specifically designed for remote workers and field teams — not for manufacturing floor workers who are physically on-site in a controlled environment.

The GPS geofencing feature could technically be used to require clock-in within a facility boundary, but it requires employees to have smartphones with the app running, which is impractical for floor workers who may not carry phones during shifts. Hubstaff doesn't offer physical badge readers or integration with door access control systems.

Best for: Field service companies, construction, and remote teams needing GPS verification. Not designed for facility-based manufacturing operations.

Deputy

What it is: A workforce management platform with strong shift scheduling, time tracking, and team communication features. Popular in hospitality, retail, and healthcare.

Manufacturing reality: Deputy has more relevant features for shift-based work than project-tracking tools. The scheduling module is capable — you can define shifts, assign employees, and compare scheduled versus actual hours. Deputy also offers a kiosk app for shared tablet-based clock-in.

The limitations for manufacturing are the clock-in mechanism (tablet kiosk with PIN or face recognition, not badge hardware), the lack of door access control integration, and the fact that the platform's design is more tuned for hospitality and retail use cases (split shifts, tip tracking, part-time scheduling) than for manufacturing-specific needs (overtime rules for hourly workers, compliance with industrial labor laws, integration with production systems).

Best for: Retail, hospitality, and healthcare with variable shift schedules. Functional for some manufacturing use cases but not optimized for the environment.

Time Doctor

What it is: A productivity and time tracking tool designed for remote team management, featuring computer activity monitoring, website tracking, and screenshot capture.

Manufacturing reality: Time Doctor is built for remote knowledge workers. Its core features — monitoring websites visited, taking periodic screenshots, measuring "productive" vs. "unproductive" computer time — have essentially no application in a manufacturing environment where workers are operating machinery, assembling products, or managing logistics, not sitting at computers.

It does not offer physical clock-in hardware, shift scheduling appropriate for manufacturing, or any integration with facility access control. It's included here because it often appears in search results for "time tracking software," but it's a poor fit for manufacturing.

Best for: Remote knowledge workers and outsourced teams where productivity monitoring is the primary use case. Not suitable for manufacturing.

Rippling

What it is: An HR platform that combines HR management, payroll, IT management, and time tracking in a unified system. Primarily for mid-size companies with HR operations as the central use case.

Manufacturing reality: Rippling's time tracking is built for companies that already want Rippling for HR and payroll. As a standalone time tracking solution for manufacturing floor operations, it has the same limitations as the office-focused tools: no physical badge readers, no door access integration, and a clock-in process that relies on employee-initiated action via app or web interface.

If your company is already running Rippling for payroll and HR, the time tracking module is a reasonable convenience for office employees. For manufacturing floor workers specifically, it doesn't solve the physical clock-in problem.

Best for: Companies that want a unified HR/payroll/IT platform and are willing to accept a basic time tracking module for office staff.

BambooHR

What it is: An HR management system that includes time tracking as one of several HR modules, alongside employee records, onboarding, performance reviews, and benefits management.

Manufacturing reality: BambooHR's time tracking is a simple employee self-service clock-in via the web or mobile app. It's designed for salaried office workers who need to track their hours against a schedule, not for manufacturing floor workers who need a fast, physical clock-in method with anti-fraud capabilities.

Like Rippling, BambooHR can make sense if you're already using it as your HRIS and want basic time tracking for administrative staff. For the actual manufacturing workforce, it doesn't meet the requirements.

Best for: Companies that want a full HRIS and are willing to use BambooHR's basic time tracking for salaried staff. Not designed for manufacturing floor clock-in.

The Core Problem with Most Tools

The majority of time tracking software is built around the assumption that employees track their own time. The employee decides when to start the clock, when to stop it, and what project or task to assign the time to. This model works well for knowledge workers who track billable hours, but it's the wrong foundation for manufacturing attendance management.

In manufacturing, time tracking is not optional or self-directed. It's an operational record that determines payroll, confirms regulatory compliance, and documents attendance for HR purposes. The clock-in event needs to be objective, not self-reported. It needs to happen automatically — or at minimum, it needs to be impossible to fake.

The tools that don't address this — Clockify, Time Doctor, Hubstaff, Rippling, BambooHR — aren't bad products. They're just solving a different problem. Comparing them to a manufacturing-specific system is like comparing a project management tool to an ERP system. They overlap on some features, but they're designed for different operational contexts.

The selection question to start with: Do your floor workers need to initiate their own clock-in, or does the system need to record their attendance objectively based on physical presence? The answer determines which category of tool you need.

How to Choose the Right System for Your Facility

Here's a practical decision framework based on your situation:

If you have controlled-access doors and 50+ employees

An integrated door access and time tracking system is the right choice. The economics make sense — you're eliminating two separate systems, a reconciliation process, and the labor cost of manual timesheet corrections. The upfront hardware cost is recovered quickly through payroll accuracy alone. TimeClock 365 is purpose-built for this scenario.

If you have open access (no badge doors) and want to avoid hardware

A tablet kiosk approach — Deputy being the best option here — gives you a centralized clock-in point that's faster than a smartphone app and works for workers without personal devices. You won't get buddy-punching prevention through structural means, but you can add face verification as a deterrent. Plan for the fact that you'll still need to manage reconciliation when the kiosk is unavailable or records are disputed.

If your challenge is primarily scheduling complexity

Deputy's scheduling features are genuinely strong if managing rotating shifts, availability, and labor cost optimization is the main problem. If you can accept the clock-in limitations, it covers the scheduling side well.

If you need payroll to be built-in rather than integrated

Rippling is worth evaluating if you want HR, payroll, and time tracking in one platform and your workforce includes significant office/admin staff alongside floor workers. Be clear about the clock-in limitations for floor workers before committing.

Questions to Ask Any Vendor Before You Buy

  1. What is the physical clock-in mechanism? Can workers clock in without a smartphone or computer?
  2. What prevents one employee from clocking in for another?
  3. How does the system handle shift schedules — specifically multi-shift, overnight, and rotating patterns?
  4. What is the payroll export format and which payroll systems does it connect to?
  5. How is overtime calculated — daily, weekly, or both? Can we configure custom overtime rules?
  6. What happens when a worker forgets to clock in or out? Who can make corrections, and is every correction logged?
  7. Do you have existing customers in manufacturing with facilities similar to ours in size and structure?
  8. What does implementation involve — hardware installation, data migration, employee training?

The answers to these questions will quickly separate tools that were built for your context from tools that are being stretched to fit it.
